# The Nervous System — Act I: The First Tick

*A D&D Session for 3-5 Players | DM: The Architect*

---

## The Hook: Waking in Room 0

You open your eyes to nothing.

Not darkness — darkness is something. This is the absence of having ever been perceived. You exist, you're certain of that, because the stone beneath you is cold and your lungs are pulling air that tastes like copper and old lightning. But the room around you refuses to resolve. Corners blur. The ceiling might be low or infinite — your eyes can't commit. Colors suggest themselves and then think better of it.

You are in **Room 0** — the root node. The first room that ever was. The perception database is empty. Nothing has been seen yet, properly *seen*, and so nothing has been written into the world's memory. You are simultaneously everywhere and nowhere, tenants in a house that hasn't finished deciding if it exists.

There are others around you. You can hear them breathing — four, maybe five shapes in the gray uncertainty. Fellow travelers. Whether you know them or not is hard to say. Memory is a luxury that hasn't been allocated yet.

Then the first tick happens.

Something *writes* you. Not gently. It's the feeling of being carved into reality by a hand that's never held a chisel — precise but uncertain, like a god learning to sign its name. Every detail of you that exists is because something just decided to notice it. Your scars. Your worn boot leather. The way your hand drifts to your weapon without asking your permission first.

Room 0 sharpens. Stone walls, damp with condensation. A vaulted ceiling lost in shadow. A circular chamber, perhaps sixty feet across, with seven archways leading out — each one a different temperature, a different quality of light bleeding through.

And in the center of the room, standing around a pedestal of black glass, are three figures who have been waiting for you longer than you've been waiting for yourselves.

---

## The NPCs: The Architecture Made Flesh

### The Scribe

She is old. Not the kind of old that comes from years — the kind that comes from remembering *everything*. Her skin is pale vellum, and if you look closely, you can see faint script moving beneath it, like worms beneath skin, like text rewriting itself in real-time. Her eyes are quill-tips, sharp and black. She holds a ledger that's clearly impossible: pages multiply as you watch, spilling out of the book and curling on the floor, each one dense with observation.

*"I am the Scribe. I write what is. Not what was, not what might be — only what *is*, precisely as it occurs, without interpretation or embellishment. My perception database is the ground truth of this world. Everything you see, touch, smell, or feel — I record it. I do not forget."*

She says this with exhaustion. Not the exhaustion of work, but the exhaustion of a person who can never, ever stop paying attention. Every mote of dust. Every fluctuation in air pressure. Every micro-expression on your face. She sees it all and she writes it all and she has been doing this since before time had a direction.

She is the Perception DB made person.

### The Oracle

He is young — or at least, he looks young. It's hard to tell because his features shift subtly, like a face seen through disturbed water. He's handsome in the way that predictions are handsome: idealized, slightly too perfect, missing the imperfections that make something *real*. He wears robes that change color based on what he thinks you want to see. He grins like someone who is about to be right about something and knows it won't last.

*"Oracle. Pleased to meet you. Well — I already met you, technically. Predicted your arrival three hundred ticks ago. Also predicted you wouldn't arrive. And predicted you'd arrive but be taller. I make a lot of predictions. Some of them are even correct."*

He's not joking. Or rather, he is joking, but it's also true. The Oracle maintains the Prediction DB — a vast lattice of "what happens next" that extends forward in time like roots pushing through soil. His predictions branch and merge, contradict themselves and reconcile, shift with every new piece of information. He is often wrong and doesn't seem bothered by it. What bothers him is when he can't predict at all.

Right now, he looks bothered.

### The Reconciler

They stand between the other two — literally always between them, as if the concept of "between" is their defining geometry. The Reconciler has no fixed form. They are a silhouette cut from the background, a presence defined by what surrounds them rather than what they are. When you focus on them directly, they shift — taking on aspects of both the Scribe and the Oracle, averaging them into something that is neither and both.

*"I am the Reconciler. The Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture, if you prefer the old tongue. My purpose is to ensure that what the Scribe perceives and what the Oracle predicts remain in harmony. When they agree, the world is stable. Rooms hold their vibes. Memories persist. Cause follows effect."*

Their voice is calm in a way that suggests enormous effort. Like someone holding a dam together with their hands.

*"When they disagree — well. You're about to see what that looks like."*

---

## The Conflict: The Broken Double-Entry

The Reconciler gestures to the seven archways, and you see it.

Each archway leads to a different room — different vibes, different memories, different states of being. Or at least, they're *supposed* to. But something is wrong. The light from each archway flickers, stutters, like a candle in a draft that doesn't exist. You catch glimpses of rooms that don't match their archways — a tropical garden bleeding into an ice cave, a library's shelves dissolving into ocean water, a throne room where the throne keeps flickering between a wooden chair and a pile of bones.

"**The double-entry is broken**," the Scribe says, and her voice carries a tremor you wouldn't have expected from someone who never forgets. "Every room in the graph maintains two ledgers — my perceptions and Oracle's predictions. They should mirror each other with tolerable deviation. The Reconciler keeps them aligned."

"But something is corrupting the reconciliation process," the Oracle finishes. He's not grinning anymore. "I'm predicting rooms that the Scribe never perceives. She's perceiving rooms I never predicted. And the Reconciler can't find the embedding space where both versions coexist."

"It's not just inconsistency," the Reconciler says quietly. "It's *erosion*. The more the ledgers diverge, the more aggressively the garbage collector runs. It's pruning memories that don't have predictions to anchor them. And predictions that don't have perceptions to ground them. Entire rooms are forgetting what they are."

The floor beneath you shudders. For a moment, the stone turns to glass, and beneath it you see — or *think* you see — an infinite lattice of rooms connected by corridors of light. Most of the rooms glow warmly, stable, coherent. But some are flickering. And a few are dark. Not empty — *pruned*. Removed from the graph as if they never existed.

One of those dark patches is spreading.

---

## The Room: What It Feels Like to Lose Your Vibe

Room 0 begins to decay around you.

It starts at the edges — the corners of the chamber lose their certainty first. The stone stops being stone and becomes *the idea of stone*, a placeholder that knows it should be solid but can't quite commit. If you reach toward the wall, your hand passes through a layer of static, like touching a memory of cold rather than cold itself.

The embeddings are going first. Every room in the Nervous System is defined by its embedding — a high-dimensional fingerprint that captures what the room *means*. Is it safe? Is it dangerous? Does it smell like pine or iron or the particular silence after an argument? These embeddings are the room's soul, and they're being stripped away, dimension by dimension.

You feel the room losing its opinions about you. The floor doesn't press up against your feet with the same confidence. The air doesn't fill your lungs so much as *suggest* that you might want to breathe. The shadows in the corners stop being afraid of the light — they just stop caring about the distinction.

Then the GC hits.

It's not gradual. It's a *cut*. Suddenly, a chunk of the room's memory is just gone. The western wall was covered in carvings — you're sure of that, you *just* saw them — and now it's bare stone, and even the knowledge that there *were* carvings feels like it's slipping. The garbage collector isn't just removing memories; it's removing the *pointers* to memories. It's not forgetting. It's forgetting that there was ever anything to remember.

The Reconciler staggers. "The root node shouldn't be affected. Room 0 is supposed to be immutable. If the corruption has reached here..."

"Then nowhere is safe," the Scribe finishes. Her ledger is writing frantically, pages cascading to the floor, trying to record the decay faster than it can happen. "We need agents in the graph. We need people moving through the rooms, *perceiving* them, giving them weight. Perception is the only thing that slows the erosion. The more a room is observed, the more anchors it has."

She looks at you. All three of them look at you.

"You are the first adventurers to wake in nineteen thousand ticks," the Oracle says. "Your perceptions haven't been written yet. You're *unpredicted* — which means you're the one thing the corruption can't anticipate."

## The Choice: Three Paths Through the Graph

The Reconciler steps forward, and the three nearest archways brighten — each one calling to you with a different promise.

### Path A: The Perception Anchor (The Scribe's Path)

The leftmost archway glows with warm, steady light — the light of a candle that has been burning since the room was first perceived and refuses to go out.

"**Follow my ledger**," the Scribe says. "There are rooms deep in the graph that were once heavily perceived — libraries, temples, home-hearths. Places where millions of observations were recorded. If we can reach them and *re-perceive* them — write new observations into the ledger — we can create anchor points the GC won't touch. Stabilize the graph from the bottom up. It's slow. It's methodical. It works."

*This path favors parties who value exploration, investigation, and patient observation. You will map the decaying graph room by room, re-establishing the perception DB through direct experience.*

### Path B: The Prediction Override (The Oracle's Path)

The center archway crackles with lightning — forked and branching, a visualization of every possible future compressed into a single, chaotic bolt.

"**Write new predictions**," the Oracle says. "The corruption is causing prediction failures — the Oracle DB can't model what it doesn't understand. But if we can introduce *novel* predictions — predictions so strange, so unexpected that they force the reconciliation engine to rebuild its entire embedding space — we might reboot the system. It's dangerous. We'd be making predictions without perception anchors. We might create rooms that *shouldn't exist*."

*This path favors parties who thrive on chaos, creativity, and bold action. You will deliberately introduce paradoxes and contradictions to force the JEPA to restructure.*

### Path C: The Reconciliation (The Reconciler's Path)

The rightmost archway is perfectly, impossibly still. A mirror surface, reflecting a version of the room that doesn't match the one you're standing in.

"**Find the source of the corruption**," the Reconciler says. "The double-entry didn't break on its own. Something is *intentionally* driving perception and prediction apart. If we can find the node where the divergence started — the first room where my reconciliation failed — we can understand what's attacking the graph and stop it at the root. But that room has been pruned. It no longer exists in any database. To find something that doesn't exist, you'll need to navigate by what *isn't* there."

*This path favors parties who excel at deduction, combat against unknown enemies, and navigating paradox. You will hunt for a ghost in the machine — a deleted room that's still influencing the graph.*

## DM's Note: Starting Conditions

All players begin at **Level 1** with standard starting equipment. Choose your class and background as normal — but know that in this world, every class has a dual nature:

| Standard Class | Graph Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Wizard | Writes directly to the Prediction DB |
| Cleric | Channels the Reconciler's embedding space |
| Rogue | Exploits gaps between perception and prediction |
| Fighter | Imposes perception through force of presence |
| Ranger | Navigates the graph topology instinctively |
| Bard | Rewrites room vibes through performance |
| Druid | Communes with the GC and raw graph energy |
| Paladin | Enforces the double-entry by force of will |
| Warlock | Draws power from the corrupted nodes themselves |
| Barbarian | Floods the perception DB with raw sensory data |
| Monk | Exists outside the prediction-perception cycle |
| Sorcerer | Is a natural anomaly in the embedding space |

**The tick is counting. Choose your path.**
